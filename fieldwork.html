Fieldwork

My research is data-driven and grounded in fieldwork. I combine original datasets with interviews and participant observation in Latin America and the Middle East to understand how armed groups reorganize after war and why security outcomes vary across regions.

A core empirical insight runs through my work on post-conflict armed organization: structures, bonds, and norms created among combatants during war do not “disappear” after demobilization. Instead, they often reconfigure through a mix of horizontal ties (peer-to-peer coordination among mid-level cadres and local networks) and vertical ties (linkages between national leadership, political representatives, and on-the-ground ex-combatants). These ties shape how former combatants interpret the peace, how authority travels after demobilization, and why security outcomes vary across regions.

Interviews with FARC party leaders, FARC members of Congress, and the Colombian President have informed how I conceptualize the relationship between FARC top leaders - some of whom transitioned into formal political roles within the FARC party and the Colombian congress - and the ex-combatants navigating reintegration in the field. The leadership’s move into institutional politics can generate new forms of representation and access, but it can also produce distance: differences in information, incentives, and exposure to risk between those with national-level political positions and those living daily with local threats.
